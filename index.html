How Audiences React to Superman Across Three Generations

This project combines computational methods and humanities-based interpretation to analyze audience reviews for Superman Returns (2006), Man of Steel (2013), and Superman (2025). We use sentiment analysis, topic modeling, and word frequency tools to explore how reactions to Superman have changed over time.

Overview

Superman has been continuously reinvented on screen, and each version says something different about what audiences expect from a hero. In this project, we analyze audience reviews for three major Superman films: Superman Returns (2006), Man of Steel (2013), and Superman (2025). Building on the technical and rhetorical skills we developed in HW4-1 and HW4-2, we moved from analyzing a single dataset to comparing how reactions shift across different eras and creative directions.

For each film, we scraped audience reviews (from IMDb for 2006 and 2013, and Rotten Tomatoes for 2025), cleaned the text, and ran sentiment analysis, term frequency analysis, topic modeling, and word cloud visualizations. This main page explains our research question, methods, and overall findings, while the individual movie pages dive into the detailed charts and interpretations for each film.

Research Question

Our central question was: How have audience reactions to Superman films changed over time, and what themes and emotions dominate those responses?

At first, each of us planned to focus on just one movie and one dataset. However, as we looked at the early results from our homework assignments, we realized that the more interesting story was comparative. Superman is not just a single character; he is a cultural symbol that gets redesigned for each new generation. That led us to expand the project from one film to three.

We hypothesized that Superman Returns (2006) would show more nostalgic and steady positivity; that Man of Steel (2013) would produce highly polarized reactions; and that Superman (2025) would divide viewers between excitement for James Gunn’s new direction and skepticism about changing such an iconic character. This research question guided our tool choices and how we interpreted the language patterns we found.

Methods

Our methods combined data collection, text cleaning, and several layers of computational analysis. First, we scraped audience reviews for each film: IMDb reviews for Superman Returns and Man of Steel, and the 200 most recent audience reviews from Rotten Tomatoes for Superman (2025). These platforms provide large, user-generated texts that reflect everyday audience reactions rather than professional critic reviews.

After collecting the data, we cleaned the text by lowercasing, removing punctuation, and filtering out English stopwords. This produced a simplified version of each review that was easier for our models to process, while still preserving key words like character names, emotional descriptors, and film-related terms. We then applied two different sentiment tools: VADER, which is sensitive to emotional intensity and slang, and TextBlob, which gives a smoother polarity score. Using both allowed us to compare “emotional spikes” with more general tonal trends.

To explore what audiences actually talk about, we used term frequency analysis to identify the most common words for each film. We then used Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling to group related words into themes, such as action, character development, nostalgia, or the wider DC universe. Finally, we generated word clouds to visualize which names, adjectives, and concepts appear most prominently in the reviews.

These methods have limitations. Online reviews are self-selected and over-represent people who feel strongly enough to post. Sentiment tools sometimes misread sarcasm or mixed feelings, and topic modeling can group words in ways that require careful interpretation. However, by combining multiple methods and cross-checking our computational outputs with close reading of sample reviews, we built a richer and more reliable picture of audience reception across the three films.

Findings

Across the three films, our analysis confirmed that audience reactions to Superman are not static—they change with tone, direction, and cultural context. Superman Returns (2006) showed a strong, consistent lean toward positive sentiment. VADER revealed a large spike near +1.0, and common words focused on “superman,” “movie,” “Lois,” “Lex,” and “character,” suggesting that many viewers appreciated its nostalgic and emotional approach.

Man of Steel (2013), by contrast, displayed more polarization. VADER showed strong spikes on both the positive and negative sides, while TextBlob indicated a mix of neutral and slightly positive reviews. Frequent terms like “action,” “story,” “Superman,” “Krypton,” and “feel” suggest that viewers were deeply engaged with the film’s darker tone, intense action, and moral complexity—sometimes in admiration, and sometimes in frustration.

For Superman (2025), early Rotten Tomatoes reviews leaned positive overall, with VADER capturing enthusiastic language about the “movie,” “story,” “character,” and James Gunn’s direction. TextBlob showed that many reviews were slightly positive or neutral, indicating that while a core group of fans responded very strongly, another group offered more measured praise or mild criticism. These patterns largely matched our hypotheses, but also revealed more steady positivity for 2006 and 2025 than we initially expected.

Integration & Reflection

This project required us to integrate humanities-style interpretation with computational tools. Close reading alone can show how one or two reviews talk about Superman, but it struggles to reveal patterns across thousands of comments. By using sentiment analysis, topic modeling, and word frequencies, we were able to see large-scale trends in how audiences describe tone, character, and story across different Superman eras.

At the same time, the computational outputs only became meaningful when we interpreted them using humanities perspectives. A spike in positive sentiment matters less by itself than when we connect it to questions like: What kind of Superman do people want? Why do some viewers react strongly against darker portrayals? How does nostalgia influence reception of newer films? In this sense, computation did not replace close reading; it extended it. It allowed us to zoom out, see the bigger picture of audience reaction, and then zoom back in to understand what those reactions say about how Superman functions as a cultural symbol today.
